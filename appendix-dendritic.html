APPENDIX A  ·  ORIGIN STORIES

The Origin of the Tasters

How a hematopoietic stem cell becomes a dendritic cell — told in the language of the town.

Far from Ileum, far from the canyon, far from the Wall and the Commensals and all the trouble that would come later, there is a place called the Marrow.

The Marrow is deep inside the great bones of the body — the hip, mostly, and some of the long bones of the legs. It is warm and dark and crowded, and it is where everyone comes from. Every soldier, every Peacekeeper, every red blood cell that carries oxygen through the rivers, every platelet that plugs a wound — they all started here, in the Marrow, as children of the Founders.

The Founders are the oldest cells in the body. They don’t look like much. Small, round, quiet. They sit in special nooks in the bone — little alcoves lined with nurse cells that feed them and keep them safe — and they do two things. They make copies of themselves, so there are always more Founders. And they make children who are not Founders — children who will leave the Marrow and become something else.

A Founder’s child is called a Progenitor, and a Progenitor is the most undecided creature in the body. It has no job. No skills. No identity. It is pure potential, like wet clay before anyone has touched it. What it becomes depends entirely on what it encounters in the next few weeks of its life.

The first fork comes early. The Progenitor drifts through the Marrow and reaches a junction — a neighborhood where the chemical signals in the fluid are different depending on which way you go. Turn one way and you enter the lymphoid quarter, where the signals favor becoming a T cell or a B cell or a natural killer. Turn the other way and you enter the myeloid quarter, where the signals favor becoming a red blood cell, a platelet, a macrophage, a neutrophil — or a Taster.

Our cell turns myeloid.

In the myeloid quarter, the choices narrow but they don’t resolve. The young cell is still not a Taster. It’s a myeloid progenitor — it could still become a macrophage, or a granulocyte, or several other things. It drifts further, and the signals in the fluid keep sorting it. There’s a particular chemical here — a growth factor called FLT3 ligand — that acts like a gentle current, pulling certain progenitors toward a specific channel. Not all of them feel it. Only the ones that have the right receptor on their surface, the FLT3 receptor, respond to the pull. Our cell has it. It follows the current.

This takes it into a small, specialized neighborhood deep in the Marrow called the DC nursery. The cells here are called common dendritic progenitors, and they have made their choice — or rather, the choice has been made for them by the accumulation of every signal they’ve encountered so far. They will become dendritic cells. They will become Tasters. They are not Tasters yet — they are pre-Tasters, still soft and unfinished — but their fate is set. They can no longer become macrophages or neutrophils or anything else. That door is closed.

In the nursery, the pre-Tasters are fed more FLT3 ligand and another compound called GM-CSF, and they begin to develop the physical features they’ll need. The long, branching arms — the dendrites that give them their name — start to extend. Their surfaces become studded with pattern-recognition receptors: molecular antennae tuned to detect fragments of bacteria, viruses, fungi, anything foreign. They are being built for sensing. Their whole architecture is optimized for reaching out, grabbing, and analyzing.

When they’re ready — still young, still not fully mature, but structurally complete enough to function — they leave the Marrow. This is a significant moment. They squeeze through the wall of a blood vessel in the bone, enter the bloodstream, and are swept away. They are now circulating pre-Tasters, riding the rivers of the body, looking for a place to land.

· · ·

And this is where the story becomes about Ileum.

Most tissues in the body recruit some Tasters. The skin gets some. The lungs get some. The liver. But the gut gets more than almost anywhere else, because the gut has the most foreign material to monitor. The pre-Tasters in the bloodstream sense homing signals — chemical gradients leaking from the gut tissue, chemokines that say “come here, we need you” — and they follow them. They exit the bloodstream through the walls of small blood vessels in the gut’s lamina propria. They have arrived.

And here is the thing that makes gut Tasters different from Tasters anywhere else in the body. When a pre-Taster lands in the skin, the local environment finishes its training one way. When a pre-Taster lands in the gut, the local environment finishes its training a completely different way.

The gut is drenched in specific chemicals that the skin doesn’t have. The stromal cells — the quiet support cells in the gut’s connective tissue — produce large quantities of retinoic acid, a derivative of vitamin A. The local environment is also rich in TGF-β, the calming compound. And the pre-Taster, still impressionable, still plastic, absorbs all of this. These signals don’t just inform the Taster — they physically reshape its internal programming. A Taster trained in retinoic acid and TGF-β becomes, by default, a tolerogenic Taster. A peacemaker. Its instinct, when it encounters foreign material, will be to present that material calmly to the recruits in the Training Grounds and release the warm, herbal pheromone — TGF-β and more retinoic acid — that turns those recruits into Peacekeepers.

This is not a choice the Taster makes. It is what the Taster is, given where it grew up. A Taster raised in the gut’s peacetime chemistry becomes a creature that promotes peace. The same progenitor, dropped into inflamed tissue full of TNF and IL-6 instead of retinoic acid, would have become something much more aggressive — a Taster that sounds alarms by default, that produces IL-23 at the slightest provocation, that trains soldiers instead of Peacekeepers.

· · ·

So the young Taster settles into Ileum. She extends her long arms through the Wall for the first time. She touches the Mucus Quarter. She encounters her first Commensal — a Faecal, probably, one of the old naturalized families — and she tastes it. Analyzes the fragments. Compares them against her pattern-recognition receptors. And because she was raised in retinoic acid, because her programming is tolerogenic, because the gut’s chemistry has made her what she is, her response is calm.

She pulls her arm back. She walks to the Training Grounds. She releases the warm pheromone. Another batch of Peacekeepers begins to form.

This is a Taster doing her job. This is what she was built for. She doesn’t know, on this first quiet day in Ileum, that the Wall will weaken, that the Paneth Guards will fail, that the signals she encounters will change, that her own chemistry will shift from calm to alarm, that the same body that trained her for peace will one day make her an instrument of war — not because she chose it, but because the pheromones around her changed, and she is, in the end, a product of her environment. She always was. From the very first fork in the Marrow, she was never making decisions. She was being shaped by whatever she touched.

That’s the part of the story that’s hard to tell to outsiders. No one in Ileum is a villain. Not even the Tasters who sound the alarm. They are doing exactly what their training prepared them to do. The tragedy is that the training changes when the environment changes — and no one asked for the environment to change.
