APPENDIX B  ·  THE INSTRUMENTS

The Conscripts and Their Indestructible Weapon

What fecal calprotectin measures, why it matters, and what a number like 4,300 means in the language of the canyon.

There is a type of cell we have not talked about yet, because in peaceful times they are barely present in Ileum. They come from the Marrow, like everyone else — myeloid quarter, same early fork as the Tasters — but where the Tasters are refined and careful, these cells are anything but.

They are called neutrophils, and they are the crudest weapon the body has.

Think of them as conscripts. They are produced in staggering numbers — more than any other immune cell in the body, by far — and they live for about a day. Sometimes less. They are not trained for nuance. They do not sample. They do not taste. They do not file reports. They are called to a location by emergency signals — a scream-chemical called IL-8 that the Wall-Folk themselves produce when they are in distress — and when they arrive, they do one thing: they dump everything they have.

A neutrophil carries bags of toxic chemicals — enzymes that dissolve tissue, reactive oxygen species that burn whatever they touch, and vast quantities of a particular protein called calprotectin. Calprotectin is the neutrophil’s signature weapon. It works by stealing metal. Bacteria need zinc and manganese to survive — these metals are essential for their enzymes, their metabolism, their ability to function — and calprotectin binds those metals and locks them away. It starves bacteria to death. It is brutally effective.

But calprotectin doesn’t care what it hits. It doesn’t distinguish between an invading pathogen and a Faecal minding its own business. It floods the area, strips the metal from everything biological in range, and the neutrophil that carried it usually dies in the process — bursting open, spilling its entire cargo into the surrounding tissue. Sometimes, in a last dramatic act, the dying neutrophil even unspools its own DNA and flings it outward like a net, tangling bacteria in a sticky web of chromatin and calprotectin. The locals call these structures traps. They are as gruesome as they sound.

In a healthy Ileum, you almost never see neutrophils. There is no IL-8 distress signal. The Wall-Folk are calm. The Tasters are calm. There is no reason to call in the conscripts. If you walked through the lamina propria on a peaceful day, you might go your whole life without encountering one.

When the détente collapses, the neutrophils are the first sign that things have gone truly wrong.

As the Wall weakens, as bacteria breach the barrier, the Wall-Folk begin to scream. Not metaphorically — they release IL-8 in quantities that were never meant for routine use. It is a chemical scream, and it carries far. Neutrophils in the bloodstream feel it, swarm toward it, squeeze through the blood vessel walls, and flood into the lamina propria by the thousands. Then the tens of thousands. They arrive, they dump their calprotectin, they die, and more arrive behind them. It is a wave of disposable violence, and it causes enormous collateral damage to the tissue — which makes the Wall-Folk scream louder, which calls more neutrophils, which dump more calprotectin, which causes more damage.

And here is the part the outside doctors figured out.

When the neutrophils die and burst in the tissue near the Wall, much of their calprotectin cargo spills outward — not just into the lamina propria, but through the damaged Wall, into the lumen, and downstream. It washes out of Ileum with everything else, down the canyon, through the colon, and eventually out of the body entirely.

Calprotectin, it turns out, is an extraordinarily tough molecule. Most proteins fall apart in the gut — the digestive enzymes and the bacterial fermentation and the acid from the Upper Towns destroy them. But calprotectin is resistant to all of it. It survives the entire journey intact. Which means that when the outside doctors collect what comes out of the canyon — when they take a sample of the stool — they can measure how much calprotectin is in it.

And that number tells them everything.

A level below 50 micrograms per gram means the canyon is quiet. The neutrophils are absent. The Wall-Folk are calm. Whatever is happening in Ileum, it is not war.

A level between 50 and 120 is borderline. Some disturbance. Maybe a minor skirmish. Worth watching.

Above 120 is elevated. The conscripts have been called. The Wall is under stress.

And a level of 4,300 — the number that came back in Harrison’s stool sample in March 2025 — is a siege. Eighty-six times the upper threshold of normal. Thousands upon thousands of conscripts pouring in, dumping their cargo, dying, being replaced. The canyon floor awash in the indestructible residue of their brief, violent service.

The doctors don’t need to send a camera into the canyon to know this. They don’t need to biopsy the Wall or interview the Tasters or count the soldiers. They just measure the calprotectin in the waste stream. It is the smoke above the city. You can’t see the fire from outside, but you can see the smoke, and the amount of smoke tells you how bad it is.

· · ·

When a doctor tells a patient that their fecal calprotectin is elevated, what they are really saying is: we counted the remains of your conscripts in what washed out of the canyon, and there are far too many of them. The war is on. The neutrophils have been called. And every microgram of calprotectin in that sample represents a dead conscript who lived for a few hours, dumped its cargo into your inflamed intestine, and was swept away — the most disposable soldier in the body, leaving behind only its indestructible weapon as proof that it was ever there.
