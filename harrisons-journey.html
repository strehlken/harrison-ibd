THE MUCOSAL QUARTERLY  ·  CASE FILE

Harrison’s Canyon

A clinical chronicle of a seven-year-old’s Crohn’s disease, told from the other side of the wall — where the doctors stand with their instruments, trying to read the smoke.

I. The first signs

Harrison never had normal stools. From infancy — six months, nine months, onward — they were loose. His parents, first-time parents, had no baseline for comparison. They didn’t know this wasn’t normal. Nobody told them. It was not until he was three, during the pandemic, that a teacher at his school pulled his mother aside and said: this is not typical.

About a week later, the stools became bloody.

Testing followed. A colonoscopy followed. In December 2020, at three and a half years old, Harrison was diagnosed with Crohn’s disease. The family history was not ambiguous: a paternal grandmother with Crohn’s, a mother with lupus. Two autoimmune conditions on two sides of the family. The genetic susceptibility was written before Harrison was born.

He was started on Pentasa — mesalamine, two capsules a day. A topical anti-inflammatory that works on the surface of the wall like a cooling spray. In the language of the canyon: it calms the Wall-Folk directly, reduces the IL-8 scream signal, slows the conscript recruitment. It is a gentle intervention. For mild disease, it can be enough.

II. The long smolder

March 2021

The first calprotectin measurement: 1,510. The smoke was already visible. The canyon had never been quiet — not in any measurement anyone had ever taken. Whatever was happening inside Harrison’s gut was generating a steady stream of dead conscripts from the very beginning of recorded observation.

Over the next year, the numbers climbed. February 2022: 2,500. The Pentasa was holding the line, but the line was moving.

May 2022

Calprotectin: 4,080. The first siege. Harrison was four. Dr. Rosen sent a camera through — a colonoscopy that found no ulcers, no additional inflammation beyond what was already known. The scope saw a canyon at war but not yet structurally damaged. The response was to increase the cooling spray: Pentasa was bumped from two capsules to three per day.

It worked, partially. By November 2022, calprotectin had dropped to 666 — the lowest value ever recorded in Harrison’s history. But 666 is still thirteen times the upper limit of normal. The canyon had never been quiet. It had only been quieter.

Through 2023 and into 2024, the numbers drifted: 1,260 in June 2023, 1,100 in December, 789 by August 2024 (Pentasa now at four capsules a day). A long smolder. The canyon at war but not in crisis. The cooling spray doing what it could, which was never enough to extinguish the fire — only to keep it from consuming the walls.

It was not enough.

III. The second siege

March 7, 2025

The calprotectin came back at 4,300. Almost identical to the first siege three years earlier. Eighty-six times the upper limit of normal. The cooling spray had been overwhelmed again. The canyon was in full-scale war — conscripts flooding in by the tens of thousands, dumping their calprotectin cargo, dying, being replaced by more. Harrison was spending more time on the toilet at night. He had vomited at school twice in two weeks, for no clear reason. He never complained of pain. He was seven years old.

March 26, 2025

Dr. Danya Rosen at Greenwich Hospital sent two cameras into the canyon. One from below — a colonoscope threaded from the rectum to the terminal ileum. One from above — an upper endoscope down the esophagus, through the stomach, into the duodenum.

The colonoscope saw what looked, on the surface, like ulcerative colitis. Diffuse granularity from rectum to cecum — a continuous band of angry, roughened mucosa, the Wall-Folk visibly inflamed across the entire colon. A few ulcers at the ileocecal valve, where the border crossing connects the colon to the terminal ileum. The terminal ileum itself looked normal through the camera. Biopsies were taken everywhere.

If this had been the only information, you might have called it UC.

But the upper scope found something UC could not explain. The duodenum — the very first segment of the small intestine, hundreds of centimeters upstream from the colon — showed necrotizing inflammatory ulceration. Ulcers in the duodenum. This is Crohn’s territory. UC does not go there. UC is a disease of the colon that starts at the rectum and extends upward but never leaves the large intestine. Crohn’s goes wherever it wants.

The biopsies came back and confirmed it. The terminal ileum — the segment the scope had called normal — showed mild chronic ileitis under the microscope. Inflammation invisible to the camera but visible to the pathologist. The canyon wall looked fine from the inside, but the foundations were already infiltrated.

Diagnosis: Crohn’s colitis with upper GI and ileal involvement

Key findings: Pancolonic granularity, ileocecal valve ulcers, duodenal necrotizing ulceration, histologic chronic ileitis in endoscopically normal ileum

IV. Reading the walls from outside

April 7, 2025

Twelve days after the scope, an intestinal ultrasound — the first attempt to read the canyon walls from the outside, without sending a camera through. Wall thickness measurements: transverse colon 0.10 cm, descending colon 0.12 cm, sigmoid colon 0.14 cm. All normal. The walls were not thickened. Color Doppler showed some scattered blood flow in the transverse colon wall — a hint of hyperemia, increased vascularity — but nothing dramatic.

In the language of the story: the Builders were still asleep. The fighting was at the surface — mucosal, superficial — but the deep structural layers of the wall, where fibroblasts live and fibrosis begins, were not yet engaged. Agreement 3 was holding.

This was the baseline. The reference point against which every future ultrasound would be compared.

June 2, 2025

The second ultrasound. Performed at the Feinstein IBD Center at Mount Sinai, by Dr. Spencer’s team. This one told a more nuanced story.

Sigmoid colon: 0.09 and 0.12 cm. Descending colon: 0.08 cm. Paper-thin. Normal.

Ascending colon: 0.26 cm. Nearly three times the left-sided measurements.

Terminal ileum: 0.29 cm. The thickest segment measured. Borderline — the standard threshold for abnormal is 3 mm, and this was 2.9 mm. The Doppler images showed scattered vascular flow in and around the ileal wall.

This was the pattern that confirmed Crohn’s from yet another angle. In UC, the thickest walls should be distal — left-sided, near the rectum. Harrison’s were right-sided, with the ileum thickest of all. The scope had seen a UC-like surface. The ultrasound saw Crohn’s-like walls. The histology had already settled it, but the ultrasound provided independent corroboration — and, more importantly, a way to follow it over time without sedation, without prep, without another scope in a seven-year-old.

V. The strategists

Harrison’s initial gastroenterologist recommended infliximab — an anti-TNF antibody and the standard first-line biologic for moderate-to-severe pediatric Crohn’s. It was a reasonable recommendation. But his family sought a second opinion, and on the advice of people close to the field, they went to Mount Sinai — to the Susan and Leonard Feinstein IBD Clinical Center, under the care of Dr. Marla Dubinsky and Dr. Elizabeth Spencer.

Dubinsky is the chief of pediatric gastroenterology at Mount Sinai and one of the most influential pediatric IBD physician-scientists in the country. Her research focuses on predictive models for disease progression — identifying which children’s canyons are at risk of structural collapse and which will respond to which interventions. She pioneered the early work on infliximab dosing in children. Spencer, her protégée, studies the genetic, microbial, and metabolomic determinants of IBD development, and specializes in the bedside intestinal ultrasound that would become the primary surveillance tool for Harrison’s walls.

The Sinai team chose a different path: risankizumab — a monoclonal antibody against IL-23. In the story’s terms: rather than lowering the temperature of the entire canyon (which is what anti-TNF does — reducing the broad environmental intensity), they chose to cut off the specific pheromone that was turning the Tasters into alarm-sounders and the recruits into soldiers. IL-23 blockade targets the inversion of Agreement 2 directly. It stops the training of new Th17 warriors at the source, while leaving the homeostatic IL-17 production — the building material the Wall-Folk need — intact. It is, in the language of the framework, a more precise intervention: not dimming the lights across the whole canyon, but silencing the specific alarm signal that was driving the military buildup.

Risankizumab is not yet approved for pediatric Crohn’s — it is approved for adults. Getting insurance to cover it for a seven-year-old required persistence. But the rationale was sound: IL-23 blockade has a mechanistic argument for deeper, more durable disease control than TNF blockade, and in a young child with decades of disease ahead of him, the goal is not just suppression but the possibility of resetting the détente — retraining the Tasters, rebuilding the Peacekeeper force, and letting the tissue forget it was ever at war.

Treatment: Risankizumab (anti-IL-23)

Rationale: Targeted suppression of the IL-23-driven Th17 expansion in a pediatric Crohn’s patient with upper GI, colonic, and ileal involvement — off-label use approved by insurance on the basis of adult efficacy data and mechanistic rationale

VI. The ceasefire

July 1, 2025

Three months into risankizumab treatment. Fecal calprotectin: 102. Borderline — above the normal threshold of 50 but well below the siege level. The canyon was responding. The conscripts were thinning out. The fire was not yet extinguished, but it was no longer raging.

November 28, 2025

Fecal calprotectin: 25.

Normal. Not borderline. Not trending down. Normal. Below the threshold of 50 that separates quiet from not-quiet. Below the lowest level ever recorded in Harrison’s entire medical history — lower than any measurement on Pentasa, lower than the 666 nadir of November 2022. For the first time since anyone had been watching, the canyon was quiet.

The complete blood count corroborated the story. White blood cells had drifted from 11.5 to 8.6 over the preceding two years — the body’s overall mobilization cooling, the Marrow producing fewer conscripts because fewer were being demanded. Eosinophils stayed low-normal throughout — 0.7 to 1.3 percent — confirming that this was never a T2 war, never an allergic or parasitic process. This was Th1/Th17 from the start. Soldiers and conscripts, not the eosinophil corps.

The risankizumab had worked. The alarm pheromone had been silenced. The Tasters were no longer training soldiers. The fighting had subsided. The conscripts had demobilized.

VII. The window

Harrison is eight now. His canyon is quiet. The calprotectin says peace. The ultrasound baseline says the Builders never woke up. The walls are thin. The architecture is preserved. There is no fibrosis. There is no stricture. There are no pseudopolyps. The Villus Towers — or in the colon’s case, the crypts and the smooth mucosal surface — are intact.

He is in the window.

The immunological conflict was real. Two sieges over five years, with a long smolder in between. A calprotectin that never once reached normal on Pentasa. But the structural ceasefire held through all of it. The Builders stayed asleep through the worst of it, and now the fighting has been suppressed before they had reason to mobilize. The tissue has not crossed into irreversible remodeling. The canal has not been entombed in scar.

The doctors at Sinai are watching the walls. That is what the serial ultrasounds are for — not to see whether the surface is inflamed (the calprotectin tells you that more cheaply), but to see whether the deep structure is changing. Wall thickness is the surveillance of Agreement 3. As long as it holds, the canyon can recover. As long as the Builders sleep, the architecture can be restored.

Everything that follows — how long the risankizumab holds, whether the disease escapes, whether the treatment needs to escalate, whether the Tasters can be retrained toward tolerance, whether the Faecals can recolonize, whether the Paneth Guards can resume their work — depends on maintaining the ceasefire long enough for the tissue to forget it was ever at war.

The peace was never really peace. But the treatment has bought time, and time is what a seven-year-old’s canyon needs most. The walls are standing. The Builders are asleep. The smoke has cleared.

The window is open.
