The Town at the Bottom of the World

A story about a walled town, the foreign market outside its gates, the peacekeepers who held everything together, and the season when the peace came apart.

Part one: The wall

The town of Ileum sat at the bottom of a long, winding canyon, and it had been there for as long as anyone could remember. It wasn't a large town. Its buildings were tall and thin — the famous Villus Towers — packed close together, their walls touching, their foundations sunk deep into the canyon floor. If you looked at the town from above, it looked like a carpet of fingers reaching up from the earth. Each tower housed families of Wall-Folk: quiet, hard-working cells who spent their lives holding hands. Literally. Each Wall-Folk cell linked arms with its neighbors through special clasps called tight junctions, forming a living wall that separated the inside of the town from the outside.

And the outside was chaos.

Beyond the Wall lay the Outer Market — a teeming, churning, unimaginably crowded bazaar of foreign creatures. Trillions of them. Bacteria, mostly, of hundreds of different species, in numbers so vast that they actually outnumbered the town's own citizens. They had been there as long as the town had. Nobody remembered who came first. Some said the foreigners had helped build the town in the first place, back in the earliest days, when the canyon was first being settled. What everyone agreed on was that the relationship was complicated.

The foreigners — the Commensals, the town called them, which roughly meant those who eat at our table — lived off the scraps that flowed down the canyon from the Upper Towns. Food that the body above had already chewed and digested passed through Ileum's territory like a river, and the Commensals fed on whatever the town couldn't absorb on its own: tough plant fibers, complex starches, things the Wall-Folk had no use for. The Commensals broke these down through fermentation, and in doing so, they produced something the town desperately needed.

They called it the Good Grease.

The Good Grease — butyrate, if you want the technical name — was the primary fuel of the Wall-Folk. They ate it. It kept them alive, kept them strong, kept them holding hands in that unbroken chain that was the Wall itself. Without the Good Grease, the Wall-Folk weakened. Their clasps loosened. Gaps appeared. So the town tolerated the foreigners, and the foreigners fed the town, and this arrangement had worked for so long that most citizens thought of it as simply the way things were, like gravity or weather.

But not everyone was comfortable with it.

Deep in the foundations of the Villus Towers, in the darkest part of the town called the Crypts, lived the Paneth Guards. They were old, serious cells, stationed at the very base of each tower, and their job was to keep the Crypts sterile. Absolutely, perfectly, completely clean. The Crypts were where the town's stem cells lived — the mothers who produced every new Wall-Folk cell — and if a foreigner ever reached the Crypts, the consequences would be catastrophic.

The Paneth Guards kept this from happening by producing a thick, foul-smelling oil that they poured into the crypt approaches. This oil — a cocktail of chemicals called defensins — killed bacteria on contact. Any foreigner who drifted too close to the crypt entrance dissolved. It was brutal, and it was effective, and it was the reason the Commensals respected the Wall's foundations even as they crowded up against its outer surface.

Between the Wall and the Commensals lay a buffer zone: the Mucus Quarter. Two layers of it, actually. The outer layer was loose and wet, and some Commensals were allowed to live in it — old families who had been there so long they were practically naturalized citizens. The Faecals, the locals called them affectionately. Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, if you wanted to be formal, but nobody was ever that formal. The Faecals were good neighbors. They produced an especially potent form of the Good Grease and they calmed the town's defenses just by being present. The inner mucus layer, though, was dense, tightly woven, and off-limits. No foreigners. No exceptions. This was the true border — the last line of soft defense before the Wall itself.

So the arrangement was: the Commensals stayed outside, or at most in the outer Mucus Quarter. The Paneth Guards kept the Crypts sterile. The Wall-Folk held hands and absorbed nutrients. The Good Grease flowed. And between the two populations — the trillions of foreigners and the citizens of Ileum — there was a kind of peace. Uneasy, conditional, and dependent on a set of institutions that most people in town never thought about.

Part two: The tasters

The most interesting people in Ileum, if you asked a young cell growing up there, were the Tasters.

Tasters were long-armed, restless, curious cells — dendritic cells, officially — who lived just inside the Wall. Their job was to find out what was happening on the other side. They did this in the most direct way possible: they stuck their arms through the Wall, reaching between the Wall-Folk (who grumbled but made room), extending their long, branching limbs out into the Mucus Quarter and even into the Outer Market, and they tasted what was there. They grabbed tiny samples of whatever they could reach — bacteria fragments, food particles, foreign proteins — and pulled them back inside for analysis.

In good times, the Tasters were the most relaxed people in town. They'd come back from a shift at the Wall with their samples and they'd shrug. "Same old stuff," they'd say. "The Faecals are fine. The usual fermenters are fermenting. Nothing to worry about." And then they'd go to the Training Grounds, where the new recruits waited, and they'd give their report.

This is where it gets important.

The Training Grounds sat in the middle of town, in the open space between the Villus Towers. This was where young, undifferentiated cells — fresh recruits who had come down from the body's central nurseries — waited to find out what they would become. A recruit, at this stage, had no identity. It was not a soldier. It was not a peacekeeper. It was a blank, waiting for instructions. And the instructions came from the Tasters.

When a Taster came back calm, she released a scent — a warm, herbal, reassuring pheromone — into the Training Grounds. The recruits who smelled it began to change. Over hours and days, they developed into Peacekeepers: patient, firm, gentle cells whose job was to patrol the town and suppress any military activity. The pheromone was a blend of two compounds — TGF-β and retinoic acid — and it was the foundation of everything that kept Ileum stable. Calm Tasters made Peacekeepers. Peacekeepers kept the soldiers quiet. Quiet soldiers meant the Commensals were safe. Safe Commensals produced Good Grease. Good Grease fed the Wall. A healthy Wall meant the Tasters stayed calm.

It was a circle. And it worked.

Part three: The soldiers

But the Training Grounds could produce something else entirely.

When a Taster came back alarmed — when she'd tasted something wrong, something unfamiliar, something that smelled like invasion rather than routine — she released a different pheromone. Sharp, acrid, unmistakable. IL-23. And the recruits who smelled that became soldiers.

The same recruit. The same Training Grounds. The same raw material. But a different pheromone, and the result was a completely different cell. Not a Peacekeeper but a Th17 warrior — aggressive, cytokine-armed, and programmed to hunt and destroy anything foreign it encountered.

In small numbers, for short bursts, this was fine. If a genuinely dangerous pathogen breached the Wall — a Salmonella, say, or an aggressive E. coli strain — the Tasters were right to raise the alarm, the recruits were right to become soldiers, and the soldiers were right to attack. The infection would be cleared, the alarm would subside, the Tasters would go back to calm reporting, and the next batch of recruits would become Peacekeepers again.

The system was designed for brief emergencies, not permanent war.

The soldiers, it should be said, were not popular in town. They were necessary, everyone agreed, but they were loud and destructive and they didn't much care what they damaged in the process of fighting. They sprayed their weapons — inflammatory chemicals — in all directions, and the Wall-Folk in the vicinity often got hurt. The Peacekeepers kept them in line. A Peacekeeper could walk up to a soldier, put a hand on its shoulder, and release a calming signal — IL-10, a gentle, almost narcotic pheromone — that put the soldier into a stupor. As long as Peacekeepers outnumbered soldiers, the military stayed controlled.

And here was the thing that made old-timers in Ileum nervous, the thing that kept the Peacekeeper captains up at night: the same recruits who became Peacekeepers could, under different circumstances, become soldiers instead. The two fates were separated by nothing more than which pheromone reached the recruit first. And there was something worse — something that the captains spoke of only in whispers. Under sustained pressure, under prolonged exposure to IL-23 and another inflammatory signal called IL-6, a Peacekeeper could turn. Lose its identity. Forget its training. Its core programming — a stabilizing gene called Foxp3 — could be chemically destabilized, and the Peacekeeper would wake up one morning and find that it had become the very thing it had spent its life suppressing. An ex-Peacekeeper turned soldier was the most dangerous kind of soldier, because it knew all the Peacekeepers' patrol routes, all their calming techniques, all their weaknesses.

This almost never happened. In a healthy Ileum, there was no reason for it to happen. The Tasters were calm. The pheromones were right. The Peacekeepers held firm. The soldiers stayed sleepy.

Almost never.

Part four: The weakening

The first sign of trouble, if you knew where to look, was in the Crypts.

The Paneth Guards had been producing less defensin oil for some time — years, maybe. Nobody knew exactly why. Some said it was hereditary: a defect in an old genetic pathway called Wnt that controlled how the Guards were trained. Others said the genes were fine but had been silenced — switched off by a chemical modification that nobody could explain. Whatever the cause, the oil was thinner. The crypt approaches, once lethally sterile, were merely unwelcoming. Bacteria that would once have dissolved on contact now lingered at the edges. A few drifted inside. Not the old, familiar Faecals from the Mucus Quarter — these were opportunists. Unfamiliar species. Bolder ones.

The Wall-Folk noticed. You couldn't not notice. There were foreigners in places foreigners had never been before. Not many. Not an invasion. Just… more than there should have been, in places they shouldn't have reached.

And the town began to change the way towns always change when they feel threatened: suspicion turned inward. The naturalized Faecals in the Mucus Quarter, who had lived peacefully alongside the Wall for generations, started getting looks. "They're foreigners too," some cells muttered. "How do we know they're not helping the others get in?" It didn't matter that the Faecals were producing more Good Grease than ever, trying to compensate, trying to keep the Wall-Folk fed and strong. A foreigner was a foreigner. The sentiment was shifting.

The Tasters noticed before anyone else. They always did — it was their job. Their long arms, reaching through the Wall, were encountering things they didn't recognize. New bacterial fragments. Unfamiliar protein signatures. Compounds that didn't match anything in their memory. They pulled back their arms and stood in the alleys between the Villus Towers with troubled expressions.

At first, they kept their reports neutral. "Increased activity," they said at the Training Grounds, releasing the usual calming pheromone. "Nothing confirmed. Monitoring." But the samples kept getting stranger. The encounters at the Wall kept getting more frequent. And the Tasters — who were, beneath their professional calm, among the most emotionally sensitive cells in Ileum — began to feel genuinely afraid.

The shift, when it came, was not a decision. Nobody voted. Nobody gave an order. It was a chemical thing — a change in the composition of what the Tasters had been absorbing through their outstretched arms, a shift in the pattern-recognition receptors that covered their surfaces. One morning, a Taster came back from the Wall and, instead of the warm herbal pheromone, she released IL-23.

Sharp. Acrid. Unmistakable.

In the Training Grounds, a batch of recruits that would have become Peacekeepers became soldiers instead.

It was one batch. Then another. Then another. The Tasters couldn't help it — their own biology was responding to what they were encountering, and what they were encountering was genuinely alarming. They were not lying. They were not exaggerating. The Wall was weakening. Foreign material was appearing where it shouldn't. The alarm pheromone was, by any objective measure, appropriate.

But appropriate or not, its effects were devastating.

Part five: The inversion

Within weeks, the balance in Ileum had shifted. More soldiers, fewer Peacekeepers. The soldiers, newly minted and aggressive, did what soldiers do: they attacked. They attacked the bacteria that had breached the Wall. They attacked the bacterial fragments in the Mucus Quarter. They attacked — and this was the first real catastrophe — the Faecals.

The Faecals had done nothing wrong. They had lived in the Mucus Quarter for generations. They had fed the Wall-Folk. They had calmed the town's defenses. But to a Th17 soldier pumped full of inflammatory purpose, a bacterium was a bacterium. The soldiers could not distinguish between a naturalized neighbor and an invading pathogen. They were not trained for nuance. They were trained for killing.

The Faecals died. And with them went their Good Grease — the high-quality, anti-inflammatory fuel that had kept the Wall-Folk strong and, crucially, had subsidized the production of new Peacekeepers. Without the Faecals' contribution, the Peacekeeper training pipeline slowed. Fewer calming signals. Fewer recruits going into the Peacekeeper track. More going into the military.

And then the thing the Peacekeeper captains had always feared: the turnings began. Under the relentless pressure of IL-23 and IL-6 flooding the town, Peacekeepers on patrol began to lose their Foxp3 programming. They'd stop mid-shift. Look at their hands. Something was different. Something had changed inside them. By the next morning they were carrying weapons.

Nobody betrayed anyone. That's the part of the story outsiders always get wrong. No one chose this. The Tasters didn't choose to sound the alarm — their biology responded to real danger. The soldiers didn't choose to kill the Faecals — they couldn't tell friend from foe. The Peacekeepers didn't choose to turn — their core programming was chemically destabilized by a pheromone environment they were never designed to withstand for this long.

Every single participant was doing what made sense given what was happening around them. And the result was a disaster.

Part six: The builders

Through all of this, one group of cells had been quiet. So quiet that the young cells in town sometimes forgot they existed.

The Builders — fibroblasts, officially — lived in the deep tissue beneath the Villus Towers, in the scaffolding and structural supports that held everything up. In good times, they did maintenance. Fixed cracks. Replaced worn-out fibers in the extracellular matrix. They were the kind of cells you never noticed until something broke, and then they showed up, fixed it, and disappeared again. They were not political. They had no opinions about the Commensals or the soldiers or the Peacekeepers. They built things. That was all.

But the fighting had been going on for a long time now, and the town was taking structural damage. The Villus Towers were cracking. The foundations were eroding. And the Builders were getting a signal they'd never encountered at this intensity before: a pheromone called TL1A, pouring off the activated soldiers and the inflamed tissue in waves.

TL1A told the Builders to build.

So they did. They woke up. They mobilized. They began depositing collagen — the structural protein that makes up the town's scaffolding — at an extraordinary rate. At first, this looked like a good thing. The town was damaged. The town needed repair. The Builders were repairing it.

But TL1A didn't stop. The inflammation didn't stop. And the Builders didn't stop. They kept depositing collagen — not the careful, architectural collagen of normal maintenance, but thick, dense, disordered scar tissue. They were filling in the spaces between the Villus Towers. Burying the Crypts. Sealing over the elegant, finger-like architecture that gave Ileum its ability to absorb nutrients. They were turning a living town into a solid, functionless mass.

And then something happened that changed everything: the Builders learned to give themselves their own orders. After weeks of sustained TL1A exposure, their internal chemistry reorganized. They began producing their own activating signals — their own TGF-β, their own survival compounds. They no longer needed the soldiers' pheromones to keep building. They had become self-sustaining.

The war could end tomorrow. Every soldier could lay down its weapons. Every Taster could go back to calm reporting. It wouldn't matter. The Builders would keep building. They had forgotten how to stop.

Part seven: What the doctors are trying now

The smarter doctors — the ones who studied Ileum's architecture instead of just counting its soldiers — eventually realized that there was no single thing to fix. The Wall was broken. The Tasters were alarmed. The Peacekeepers were outnumbered or turned. The soldiers were attacking everything. The Builders were self-sustaining. The Faecals were mostly gone. The Gate — the MAdCAM-1 immigration checkpoint in the blood vessels below town, where new cells entered Ileum from the wider body — was processing a steady stream of fresh soldier reinforcements, waved through on their α4β7 credentials.

No single intervention could fix this. But maybe three could.

Block the Tasters' alarm pheromone — IL-23 — and you stop them from training new soldiers. The existing soldiers age out. The recruit pipeline shifts back toward Peacekeepers. Slowly, the balance tips. This doesn't fix the Wall. It doesn't stop the Builders. But it addresses the root cause of the army's growth.

Block TL1A — the signal that keeps the Builders in permanent mobilization — and you slow the fibrosis. Maybe, if you catch it early enough, before the Builders have learned to give themselves orders, you can actually stop them and let them return to normal maintenance. This is the one with a time limit. Once the Builders go autonomous, this intervention is too late.

Close the Gate — block α4β7, restrict the immigration checkpoint — and you stop new soldiers from arriving. The resident army is large but finite. Without reinforcements, the Peacekeepers have a chance to reassert control. The town can begin to manage its own military again.

All three. At the same time. And early — before the Builders forget how to stop, before the Villus Towers are buried in scar, before the Crypts are sealed, before the Paneth Guards' last reserves of defensin oil are gone.

That's what the doctors are trying now.

· · ·

Ileum is quieter today than it was at the height of the fighting, but it is not at peace. The Lower Quarter, where the Paneth Guards first weakened, is largely scar tissue now. The Villus Towers there are gone. The Wall-Folk who survived have reorganized into a flat, featureless layer — functional enough to keep the canyon floor covered, but nothing like the elegant, absorptive architecture that was there before. The Faecals are gone from that district entirely. The Crypts are sealed.

In the Upper Quarter, where the Paneth Guards held out longer, there is still something recognizable as a town. The Tasters are calmer — the IL-23 blocker has helped. Fewer soldiers are being trained. The Peacekeepers are slowly rebuilding their numbers. The Gate is partially closed. It's not a victory. It's a managed decline in hostilities, with everyone watching the Builders nervously, hoping the TL1A blocker reached them in time.

A young Wall-Folk cell in the Upper Quarter, born after the worst of the fighting, might grow up thinking this is normal. She holds hands with her neighbors, eats what Good Grease trickles in from the remaining Commensals, watches the occasional soldier patrol past. She has never seen a Faecal. She has never seen a functioning Villus Tower in the Lower Quarter. She doesn't know what Ileum looked like when the détente held — when the Wall was thick, the Crypts were sterile, the Tasters were relaxed, and the Peacekeepers outnumbered the soldiers ten to one.

She doesn't know what was lost. She only knows what is.

Author's note: Ileum is real. It is the last section of your small intestine, about ten feet long, and it is where most Crohn's disease begins. Every character in this story corresponds to a real cell type, every pheromone to a real cytokine, and every event to a real immunological mechanism. The names have been changed to protect the innocent. The Faecals would appreciate it if you ate more fiber.

The barrier compact
Epithelial cells maintain physical separation. Paneth cells sterilize the crypt. Commensals stay luminal. In return, commensals get habitat and protection. The DMZ reduces information flow — the truce depends partly on managed ignorance.

The tolerance pact
Tregs actively suppress effector responses to commensal antigens. DCs are conditioned to be tolerogenic. Commensals subsidize their own policing via butyrate → Treg differentiation. When the barrier weakens and antigen load rises, DCs flip from tolerogenic (TGF-β → Tregs) to inflammatory (IL-23 → Th17). The pact doesn't just fail — it inverts.
Enforced by: Tregs, IL-10, TGF-β, tolerogenic DCs, commensal butyrate
Inverted by: barrier breach → DC reprogramming → IL-23 production → Th17 differentiation

The structural ceasefire
Fibroblasts remain quiescent. Repair activates only transiently after acute injury. When chronic inflammation produces sustained TL1A/TGF-β, fibroblasts activate into myofibroblasts and acquire autocrine survival loops. They become a new autonomous player who refuses to disband — a militia with institutional momentum.
Enforced by: absence of chronic activating signals
Broken by: chronic TL1A/TGF-β → fibroblast autonomy → irreversible fibrosis
The coordination trap
Barrier weakens → more antigen pressure on tolerance → tolerance inverts (Tregs replaced by Th17) → chronic inflammation → fibroblast activation → structural remodeling → further barrier damage → more antigen → … Each player is behaving rationally given what every other player is doing. No one can unilaterally return to the healthy equilibrium. The disease state is self-maintaining. Escaping the trap requires changing the payoff matrix for multiple players simultaneously — which is the mechanistic argument for combination therapy and early intervention before fibroblasts become autonomous.
